# IRS Guidance: Home Cost Basis, Capital Improvements & Repairs

Primary sources: IRS Publication 523 (*Selling Your Home*), Publication 530 (*Tax Information for Homeowners*), and general basis rules under IRC §1012/§1016.

## The core distinction

**Cost basis** = purchase price + qualifying settlement/closing costs + capital improvements − any basis adjustments (casualty loss reimbursements, depreciation if a home office/rental was claimed, energy credits that required a basis reduction).

Only **capital improvements** increase basis. **Repairs and maintenance** do not, regardless of cost.

| | Capital Improvement (adds to basis) | Repair / Maintenance (does not add to basis) |
|---|---|---|
| Test | Adds value, prolongs useful life, or adapts the property to new uses | Keeps the property in ordinary, efficient operating condition |
| Durability | Becomes a permanent part of the home or is installed with lasting intent | Restores something to its prior working state |
| Examples | Room addition, new roof, kitchen remodel, new HVAC system, new windows/doors, permanent landscaping/hardscaping (patio, retaining wall, in-ground irrigation), new flooring, solar panel installation, septic system replacement, new plumbing/electrical systems, built-in appliances | Painting, fixing a leak, patching a roof, replacing a broken window pane, routine landscaping (mowing, mulching, seasonal planting), pest control, cleaning, HVAC servicing |

**Important nuance**: An item that looks like a "repair" in isolation can become part of a capital improvement if it's done as part of a larger renovation project (e.g., repainting as part of a full room remodel). Conversely, a single expensive replacement of a like-for-like broken component (e.g., swapping a broken water heater for an equivalent new one) is generally a repair, not an improvement — the test is whether it adds value/life or merely restores prior condition.

## What does NOT belong in home cost basis

- **Personal property / furnishings**: furniture, area rugs (not wall-to-wall installed carpet), curtains/drapes, decor, appliances that are freestanding (not built-in), art. These may be relevant for personal property insurance or separate tax purposes, but they are not part of the *home's* basis.
- **Repairs and maintenance**: see above.
- **Routine landscaping upkeep**: mowing, seasonal plantings, mulch, pest control, lawn treatment.
- **Financing costs**: mortgage interest, most loan origination fees (a few closing costs at purchase do count toward original basis, but that's a one-time event, not ongoing).

## Record-keeping requirements

The IRS puts the burden of proof on the taxpayer to substantiate basis. For each capital improvement, best practice is to retain:
1. **Itemized receipt or invoice** (not just a credit card statement line) showing what was purchased/done.
2. **Proof of payment** (statement, cancelled check, bank record).
3. **Contract or scope of work** for larger projects (helps establish that a "repair-looking" line item was part of a capital project).
4. **Before/after photos** for renovations, where practical — not required, but strengthens the record in an audit.

Keep records for as long as the home is owned, **plus at least 3 years after the return reporting the sale is filed** (the standard statute of limitations), longer if substantial understatement or fraud exposure exists.

## Home sale gain exclusion context

Under IRC §121, an individual can exclude up to $250,000 of gain ($500,000 married filing jointly) on the sale of a primary residence if ownership/use tests are met. Cost basis tracking still matters even under the exclusion because:
- Gain above the exclusion is taxable.
- The exclusion amounts are not indexed for inflation, so in high-appreciation markets accumulated capital improvements can be the difference between a fully excluded gain and a taxable one.
- If the home is ever converted to rental/business use, or ownership/use tests aren't met, the full adjusted basis matters immediately.

## Practical classification heuristic used by this skill

When categorizing a transaction, ask:
1. **Is it home-related at all?** If not (travel, groceries, personal shopping), exclude entirely — don't add clutter to the ledger.
2. **Is it home-related but personal property/furnishings** (freestanding furniture, rugs, decor, curtains)? Exclude from basis, but optionally log separately since Samantha may still want the record for insurance/personal property purposes.
3. **Is it home-related and either (a) clearly a capital improvement** (permanent fixture, structural, systems replacement, renovation-scope purchase) **or (b) clearly routine repair/maintenance** (pest control, cleaning, lawn care, small hardware)? Classify accordingly with a confidence note.
4. **Is it ambiguous** (could be a repair or could be part of a larger capital project, e.g., paint, small hardware, tile in unclear quantity)? Flag as **Needs Review** rather than guessing — the difference often depends on context the credit card statement won't show (was this part of a bathroom gut renovation, or a $12 replacement hinge?).

This skill is not a substitute for advice from a CPA or tax attorney on any specific item; it's a disciplined first-pass system to make sure nothing gets lost and the eventual basis calculation has good underlying records.
